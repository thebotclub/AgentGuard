Are Your Agents In Scope?

The first question GRC teams ask is: "Does the EU AI Act apply to us?" If you're deploying AI agents that interact with EU users or affect EU citizens, the answer is almost certainly yes — and the relevant provisions are broader than most compliance teams initially assume.

The EU AI Act applies to any AI system that is placed on the EU market, used in the EU, or operated by a non-EU entity where outputs affect EU users. This includes US and UK companies with EU-facing products or EU employee deployments.

The Act's Annex III defines high-risk AI systems by sector and use case. AI agents are most likely classified as high-risk when operating in:

The critical distinction for agents: An AI system that autonomously takes actions — sends emails, calls APIs, executes transactions, modifies records — raises the risk classification bar compared to a system that only provides recommendations. The degree of autonomy is a key factor regulators evaluate.

📋 Key Question for Your Risk Assessment

Does your AI agent make or implement decisions that have a significant effect on people's lives, employment, access to services, or safety? If yes, you are in high-risk territory. Consult your legal team on the specific classification — but build the technical compliance infrastructure now.

The Five Articles That Matter Most for Agentic AI

The EU AI Act contains 113 articles. Five of them — Articles 9, 12, 13, 14, and 15 — are where most agentic AI compliance requirements live. Here is what each requires and what it means in practice for agent deployments.

Article 9

Risk Management System

Requires providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to establish, implement, document, and maintain a risk management system — throughout the entire lifecycle of the AI system.

What this means for agents
  • A documented risk identification process for each agent deployment
  • Residual risk estimation and mitigation measures
  • Testing procedures to evaluate agent behaviour against known risks
  • Continuous monitoring — not a one-time assessment
  • Updates when the agent's scope, tools, or behaviour changes

Policy engine provides declarative risk controls per agent; continuous monitoring surfaces risk events in real time; version-controlled policies create an auditable risk management trail

Article 12

Record-Keeping & Logging

High-risk AI systems must automatically generate logs that enable post-hoc review of the system's operation. These logs must be kept for at least 6 months (or longer where required by other legislation).

What this means for agents
  • Every agent action must be logged — not just final outputs, but the reasoning chain
  • Logs must identify the period of each use, the input data, and the output produced
  • For agents with human oversight gates: log who approved what, when
  • Logs must be tamper-evident — regulators can challenge chains of custody
  • The logging system itself must be part of conformity assessment

Tamper-evident, cryptographically chained audit logs capturing every agent action, tool call, input, and reasoning chain — with 6-month+ retention and export in auditor-ready formats

Article 13

Transparency & Information Provision

High-risk AI systems must be sufficiently transparent so that deployers can understand, interpret, and use the system appropriately. This includes instructions for use and information about capabilities and limitations.

What this means for agents
  • Document what the agent can and cannot do — its declared scope
  • Provide human-readable documentation of the agent's decision-making logic
  • Disclose when an AI agent is making or influencing a decision (to affected persons)
  • Maintain up-to-date technical documentation as the agent evolves

Policy-as-code provides human-readable agent scope documentation; audit dashboard makes agent behaviour interpretable to deployers and reviewers

Article 14

Human Oversight

High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed to be effectively overseen by natural persons. This means deployers must be able to understand, monitor, and intervene in agent operations.

What this means for agents
  • Ability to immediately halt agent operations (kill switch requirement)
  • Human review gates for high-risk actions before execution
  • Real-time monitoring so operators can identify and act on anomalies
  • Evidence that human oversight is not just possible but actually practiced
  • "Automation bias" mitigation — humans must be genuinely informed, not rubber-stamping

Kill switch with <500ms response time; configurable human-in-the-loop gates for high-risk actions; real-time risk scoring dashboard; HITL approval logs for evidence

Article 15

Accuracy, Robustness & Cybersecurity

High-risk AI systems must achieve appropriate levels of accuracy and be resilient to errors, faults, and adversarial inputs — including prompt injection and data poisoning attacks.

What this means for agents
  • Documented performance metrics and accuracy baselines
  • Adversarial testing — evidence that the agent has been tested against known attacks
  • Resilience to prompt injection and data poisoning (explicitly cited in guidance)
  • Incident response procedures for when the agent is compromised
  • Continuous performance monitoring, not just pre-deployment testing

Runtime anomaly detection for adversarial inputs; prompt injection prevention; continuous performance monitoring; incident response playbooks; forensic replay capability

Specific Compliance Requirements Agents Create

Beyond the specific articles, deploying AI agents creates several compliance requirements that traditional IT systems don't generate. These are the gaps most companies discover only when the auditor arrives.

The "Reasoning Chain" Problem

Traditional IT systems log what they did. AI agents need to log why they did it. Regulators — and courts — are beginning to require that organisations explain AI-assisted decisions at a granular level. An agent's chain-of-thought is the only evidence of this reasoning.

Most logging infrastructure captures API calls and responses. It does not capture what was in the agent's context window at decision time — which retrieved documents it was referencing, which prior steps influenced the current decision, or what internal "reasoning" the model produced. Without chain-of-thought logging, you cannot reconstruct why an agent did what it did.

Multi-Agent Attribution

When multiple agents collaborate — an orchestrator delegates to sub-agents, which use tools and pass results back — it becomes very difficult to attribute responsibility for a specific outcome. Under Article 12's logging requirements, you need to be able to trace any action back to the specific agent, context, and decision point that initiated it, even in complex multi-agent pipelines.

The "High-Risk Action" Definition Problem

Article 14 requires human oversight for high-risk actions — but most organisations haven't formally defined which agent actions constitute "high-risk." This needs to be a documented, version-controlled definition, not an informal understanding. Auditors will ask for it.

⚠ Common Compliance Gap

We consistently find in discovery interviews that companies can produce logs of what their agents did, but not why, who approved it, or what data was in scope at decision time. Auditors are specifically trained to look for these gaps. The organisations that pass audits have this evidence before the auditor asks for it.

What Auditors Will Ask For

Based on EU AI Act conformity assessment guidance and regulatory audit frameworks across GDPR, DORA, and NIS2 (which provide the audit methodology template), here is what auditors will request when evaluating your agentic AI deployment:

AreaWhat Auditors Will RequestMost Companies Have
Risk Documentation Formal risk assessment per agent deployment, dated and version-controlled Informal notes, if anything
Logging Tamper-evident logs of every agent action, 6+ months, searchable by agent ID, date, and action type Application logs in CloudWatch / Splunk that capture API calls but not reasoning
Human Oversight Evidence Records of human approval for high-risk actions; logs showing the kill switch has been tested A system prompt saying "ask before doing X"
Policy Documentation Written policy defining what each agent can and cannot do, signed off by a responsible person System prompts and informal developer assumptions
Incident History Log of all policy violations, anomalies, and incidents — and what was done about them Bug tickets, if escalated
Adversarial Testing Evidence that the system was tested against prompt injection and other attack vectors Functional testing only
Change Management History of changes to agent scope, tools, model version — with impact assessment Git commit history (incomplete)
💡 The Audit Principle

Auditors don't expect perfection. They expect evidence of a functioning system. The organisations that fail audits are those that have no evidence — not those whose evidence reveals manageable gaps. Build the evidence trail now, even if the controls are not yet perfect.

Timeline: What Needs to Be Done by When

August 2024
EU AI Act Entered Into Force

The Act became legally binding. Prohibition on unacceptable-risk AI systems (Art. 5) immediately applicable.

February 2025
Prohibited Practices Enforced

Articles 5 and 50 (prohibited AI practices) became enforceable. AI literacy obligations for staff began.

August 2025
GPAI Model Obligations Active

General-Purpose AI (GPAI) model obligations including transparency and copyright rules became applicable. If you use foundation models to power agents, your providers' compliance status now matters for your risk posture.

August 2026 ← YOU ARE HERE
Full High-Risk AI System Compliance Required

All obligations for high-risk AI systems (Art. 9–15, 17–20) become applicable. This is the deadline for complete technical compliance — risk management systems, logging, human oversight controls, conformity assessment documentation. Penalties: up to €30M or 6% of global annual turnover.

August 2027
Annex I High-Risk Systems Compliance

AI systems in safety components of products under existing EU product safety legislation (medical devices, machinery, aviation) become subject to full obligations.

Ongoing
Continuous Compliance Monitoring Required

EU AI Act compliance is not a one-time certification — it requires continuous monitoring, incident reporting (within 15 business days of discovering a serious incident), and annual reporting to national competent authorities.

EU AI Act Agent Compliance Checklist

Use this checklist to assess your current compliance posture. This is the minimum bar — your legal team should validate your specific situation.

EU AI Act Agent Compliance Checklist

📋 Article 9 — Risk Management

Inventory of all AI agent deployments, with use case documentation
High-risk classification assessment for each agent (completed, dated, signed)
Formal risk assessment per high-risk agent, with mitigation measures documented
Written policy defining what each agent can and cannot do (version-controlled)
Process for updating risk assessment when agent scope or tools change
Testing procedures documented and evidence of execution

📝 Article 12 — Logging & Record-Keeping

Every agent action logged with timestamp, agent ID, action type, input, and output
Reasoning chain / chain-of-thought captured (not just API calls)
Logs are tamper-evident (cryptographic integrity checking)
Log retention policy: minimum 6 months, longer where required by sector regulation
Logs are searchable and exportable in auditor-ready formats
Multi-agent pipelines: attribution chain traceable per action to initiating agent

👁 Article 13 — Transparency

Technical documentation per agent: capabilities, limitations, intended use
Instructions for use documentation available to deployers
Disclosure mechanism: affected persons informed when AI makes/influences decisions
Documentation kept current as agent evolves (change management process exists)

🛑 Article 14 — Human Oversight

Kill switch capability tested and documented (response time measured)
Formal definition of "high-risk actions" requiring human approval
Human-in-the-loop approval gates implemented for high-risk actions
HITL approval decisions logged (who approved, when, what information they had)
Real-time monitoring dashboard: operators can see what agents are doing
Evidence that human oversight is practiced — not just possible

🔒 Article 15 — Robustness & Cybersecurity

Adversarial testing conducted: prompt injection, data poisoning, goal drift
Testing results documented (including failures and mitigations applied)
Continuous performance monitoring vs. established baselines
Incident response procedures for agent compromise or anomalous behaviour
Supply chain security: model provider and framework versions tracked

📁 General Compliance Infrastructure

Responsible AI Officer or equivalent designated
Incident reporting process: serious incidents reportable within 15 business days
Staff AI literacy training documented (Art. 4)
Conformity assessment documentation package prepared
Data governance: personal data used for AI training/operation under GDPR Article 22 provisions assessed
